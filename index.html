01 — Stance

There is no federal standard for AI literacy. The workforce is figuring it out alone.

Some offices import corporate training from Google, Microsoft, or Amazon — designed for technologies that haven't cleared your network. Others watch shadow AI fill the gap, one personal subscription at a time. Neither is a strategy. Workers deserve vetted knowledge transfer from someone who understands both the environment and the mission.

02 — AI Training

Five things the federal workforce needs to know.

01

The theory of agentic AI

What an agent is, what it isn't, and why the distinction matters before you let one touch a workflow. Where autonomy lives, where it shouldn't, and how to recognize a tool that's pretending to be one.

02

Deterministic prompting

Writing instructions that produce the same result twice. The difference between asking a model a question and giving it a procedure. How to test, version, and hand off prompts the way you'd hand off code.

03

The limits of AI

Where models confabulate, where they fail silently, and what is and isn't safe to delegate. A clear-eyed map of the failure modes — so your team knows when to trust output and when to throw it away.

04

Human-in-the-loop workflows

Designing processes that keep accountability with people while taking the speed gains models offer. Checkpoints, reviewer roles, and the paperwork that proves your humans were actually in the loop.

05

Coding agents as project managers

Using coding agents to coordinate, review, and document work — not to replace developers. A management posture, not a headcount strategy. The right framing for technical leads who have to answer for what the machine produced.

03 — Product

Candid Sail

Candid Sail is a decision support tool for stress-testing plans and ideas to identify blind spots and missed opportunities. The system orchestrates a moderated debate between multiple frontier models to highlight uncertainty and weakness.
